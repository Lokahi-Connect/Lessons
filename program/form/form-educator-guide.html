Foundational Sequencing Principle

The following principle guides how Lokahi Connect sequences word-forming elements within and across all developmental bands. It is the instructional foundation for every <form> resource in this library.

"Word complexity is dependent on the number and familiarity with morphemes. Foundational sequencing reflective of word forming elements will typically follow the order: base (free), suffixes <s> <es> <ed> <ing>, suffixing conventions, prefixes and expansion of suffixes (<y> <ly> <er> <ion>), intro to etymology, base (bound), etymology expanded, chameleon prefixes, connecting vowels. But this can change with student interest, so teachers must be prepared to teach concepts as students notice and become aware of the meaningful structures." — Steph, Lokahi Connect · Meaning-First Literacy™
⚠️ Critical Reminder This sequence is a default scaffold, not a rigid script. Student noticing drives instruction. When a learner notices a prefix, a connecting vowel, or an unfamiliar morpheme — that is the moment to teach it, regardless of where it falls in the sequence. Responsive teaching is a core Meaning-First commitment.

The Foundational Sequence

The sequence below maps word-forming elements in their typical instructional order. Each stage builds on the one before — but teachers hold this sequence lightly, ready to move in response to learner awareness.

1
Free Base Band 1+
Begin with a base that can stand alone as a word. Anchor meaning before introducing any affixes. Students explore the base in oral language and connect it to print.
form play help jump
2
Foundational Suffixes: <-s> <-es> <-ed> <-ing> Band 1–2
Introduce suffixes as meaning markers, not just spelling patterns. <-s> marks plurality or third person; <-ed> marks prior time; <-ing> marks ongoing action. Word sums make the structure visible.
form + s → forms form + ed → formed form + ing → forming
3
Suffixing Conventions Band 2
When suffixes join bases, spelling sometimes changes at the join. Teach these as named conventions — not exceptions. Check the Join! routines make this investigable and memorable.
Replace: make + ing → making Double: hop + ing → hopping Change: happy + ness → happiness
Note: <form> requires no join change with foundational suffixes — this makes it ideal for early word sum work.
4
Prefixes & Expanded Suffixes Band 2–3
Introduce prefixes as meaning modifiers that attach before the base. Expand suffix repertoire to include <-y> <-ly> <-er> <-ion>. Students begin constructing and analyzing multi-morpheme words.
re + form → reform in + form → inform form + al → formal form + ation → formation
5
Introduction to Etymology Band 3
Etymology connects spelling to history and meaning. Students learn that <form> comes from Latin forma — shape, structure, arrangement. This expands word family awareness and builds academic vocabulary bridges.
Latin: forma formation transformation deformation
6
Bound Bases Band 3+
Some bases cannot stand alone — they require an affix to function as a word. Students who are secure with free bases are ready to investigate bound bases and the families they generate.
<struct> → structure <port> → transport <rupt> → disrupt
7
Etymology Expanded Band 3+
Students investigate etymology across multiple word families, noticing patterns across Latin and Greek roots. They build cross-family connections and begin to use etymology as an independent meaning-making strategy.
Latin families Greek families Cross-family connections
8
Chameleon Prefixes Band 3+
Some prefixes change their spelling to match the base they attach to — these are chameleon prefixes (also called assimilated prefixes). Teaching this explicitly demystifies spelling patterns students encounter in academic vocabulary.
in– → il– ir– im– con– → col– cor– com– ad– → ac– af– ag–
9
Connecting Vowels Band 3+
Connecting vowels (typically <o> or <i>) link morphemes in compound and Latinate words. Students investigate their function and learn to identify them in multi-morpheme words.
therm + o + meter bi + o + logy agr + i + culture

Responsive Teaching: Following the Learner

The sequence above describes a typical progression — not a required one. Meaning-First Literacy™ is grounded in the principle that learner noticing is a legitimate entry point to any stage of the sequence.

📌 What this looks like in practice A Band 1 student notices the word performing on a poster and asks "Is per a part?" — that is the moment to introduce prefixes, even if the class has not reached that stage. A Band 2 student asks "Why does information have form in it?" — that is the moment to introduce etymology. Student curiosity is instructionally valid at every stage.
Teacher readiness
Know the full sequence so you can meet students wherever their noticing takes them. You don't have to teach everything at once — but you should be able to respond to anything.
Linguistically accurate scaffolding
When responding to student noticing outside the planned sequence, maintain precision. Simplify access — never simplify truth. Use correct terminology even with young learners.
