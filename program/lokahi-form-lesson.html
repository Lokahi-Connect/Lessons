What other words are related?

The base <form> travels with many different prefixes and suffixes. Each one shifts the meaning in a specific direction. Let's map the whole family.

Prefixes that travel with <form>

<in->
Meaning: into; to put into form
in + form → inform · in + form + at + ion → information
Etymology: Latin in- (into, upon)
<re->
Meaning: again; back

<trans->
Meaning: across; through; beyond
trans + form → transform · trans + form + at + ion → transformation

<per->
Meaning: through; thoroughly

<uni->

To form together with something else; to match a shape or rule

Activity 4 of 5 ~10 min

Read like a Word Scientist

As you read this passage, look for words from the <form> family. The base <form> is highlighted in teal. Prefixes and suffixes are highlighted in gold.

Every living thing has a form. A bird's wings, a spider's web, the rings inside a tree — nature is always forming structures that solve problems.

Scientists who study how these structures develop are called formation biologists. They want to understand how a simple cell can transform into a complex living body. The word transformation captures exactly what happens: a complete change across forms.

Information about how shapes develop helps scientists formulate new ideas. When researchers share their findings, they inform the entire scientific community.

Even the way scientists work has a form. The scientific method is a kind of uniform process — every scientist follows the same structured steps. This lets researchers reform old ideas when new evidence appears.

A scientist who presents findings at a conference must perform their research clearly. A great performance means the audience is well informed.

Activity 5 of 5 ~8 min

How do the graphemes function?

Now that we understand the meaning and structure of the <form> family, we can look carefully at how the graphemes (spelling units) represent sounds in context.

A grapheme is a spelling unit — a letter or group of letters that represents a phoneme in a specific word. We describe graphemes in context, not in isolation.
<f>
In form, inform, reform, transform: The grapheme <f> represents the phoneme /f/. This is stable across the whole <form> family — the spelling never shifts because the morpheme carries a consistent meaning.
<o>
In form: The grapheme <o> represents the phoneme /ɔː/ (as in "more"). Notice that this vowel sound is consistent in form, formal, formation, inform, perform, transform, uniform — the spelling stays the same because the morpheme stays the same.
<ti>
In formation, information, transformation: The grapheme <ti> represents the phoneme /ʃ/ before <on>. This is a positional function — <ti> before <on> consistently marks the /ʃ/ sound in Latinate words. Not a "rule-breaker" — a predictable pattern in this morpheme context.
<con->
In conform, conformation: The prefix <con-> (together, with) has an allomorph <com-> before bases starting with /m/, /b/, /p/. In <conform>, the base begins with /f/, so the prefix stays as <con->. This is not an exception — it's a predictable phonological accommodation.
