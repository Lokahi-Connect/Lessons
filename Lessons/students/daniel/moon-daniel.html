What is the Moon?
The Moon is a satellite — an object in space that orbits something else. It orbits Earth. The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite.
🔭
The word satellite
The base comes from Latin satelles — meaning attendant or companion. The Moon is Earth's companion in space. We also use satellite for human-made objects we launch into orbit.

Stop 1 — The Surface

The Moon looks grey and rocky from Earth. Up close, it is one of the most dramatic landscapes in the solar system.

🔊 Read this section aloud

Three things you would see

The Moon's surface from above — you can see the dark flat areas and bright rocky regions.

⬛
Maria — dark flat plains
Maria (MAH-ree-ah) are huge flat basins on the Moon. They look dark. The word maria comes from the Latin word for seas — early astronomers thought they were oceans. There is no water in them.
⛰️
Highlands — bright rocky areas
Highlands
are high, rocky, mountainous areas on the Moon. They look bright. They are older and rougher than the maria.
💥
Craters — bowl-shaped holes
Craters are circular holes caused by rocks from space — called asteroids and comets — slamming into the surface. The Moon is covered in them.

The footprint that is still there

Apollo 11, 1969 — an astronaut's bootprint in the Moon's surface dust. NASA public domain.

👣
Regolith — the Moon's "soil"
Regolith is a layer of fine powder and broken rock that covers the Moon's surface. It was created by billions of years of space rocks hitting the surface and grinding it into dust. The footprint in this photo is still on the Moon today — there is no wind or rain to erase it.

🔭 Word noticing — highland

🔬
Built from two free bases
The word highland is made of two parts: high + land. Each part carries its own meaning. High = elevated. Land = ground. Together: elevated ground. This kind of word is called a compound word.
high + land → highland

📏
What is mass?
Mass is how much matter — how much stuff — an object is made of. The Moon has less mass than Earth. Less mass means less gravity. Gravity is the pull that holds you to the ground.
6️⃣
Six times weaker
The Moon's gravity is about 6 times weaker than Earth's. That means if you weigh 60 pounds on Earth, you would only weigh 10 pounds on the Moon.

Apollo astronauts really did bounce

Buzz Aldrin on the Moon, Apollo 11, 1969. NASA public domain.

🚶
Why astronauts moved slowly
The astronauts on the Moon wore heavy spacesuits. But because of low gravity, they still felt lighter than on Earth. They moved in slow, bouncy strides. Every step sent them floating slightly upward before coming back down.

Stop 3 — Moon Phases

Every night the Moon looks a little different. That's because of light — not because the Moon is changing shape.

🔊 Read this section aloud
Tap to start · tap again to stop

Why the Moon looks different each night

The 8 phases of the Moon — one complete cycle takes about 28 days.

☀️
The Moon does not make its own light
The Moon reflects light from the Sun. When the Sun shines on the Moon, one side is bright. The other side is dark. As the Moon travels around Earth, we see different amounts of the bright side. That is what creates the phases.
🔄
One cycle = about 28 days
The Moon takes about 28 days to complete one full orbit around Earth. That is why the phases repeat every 28 days — roughly once a month.

🔭 Word noticing — illuminate

💡
To illuminate = to light up
The Sun illuminates the Moon. Illuminate comes from Latin lumen — meaning light. You can see the same base in: illuminated, luminous, luminescence. The base always carries the idea of light.
illuminate · illuminated · luminous · luminescence

Stop 4 — Eclipses

An eclipse happens when the Sun, Earth, and Moon line up in just the right way. They are rare — and spectacular.

🔊 Read this section aloud
Tap to start · tap again to stop

Two kinds of eclipse — tap to explore

A total solar eclipse — the Moon completely blocks the Sun. Rare and dramatic.

🌑
Solar eclipse — the Moon blocks the Sun
During a solar eclipse, the Moon moves directly between the Sun and Earth. The Moon's shadow falls on Earth. It gets dark outside — even in the middle of the day. This can only happen during a new moon.
❓
Why doesn't it happen every month?
The Moon's orbit is tilted at a slight angle compared to Earth's orbit around the Sun. Most months, the Moon passes above or below the Sun from our view — not directly in front of it. A solar eclipse only happens when the alignment is exact.

A total lunar eclipse — the Moon turns red as it passes through Earth's shadow.

🔴
Lunar eclipse — Earth blocks the Sun's light
During a lunar eclipse, Earth moves between the Sun and the Moon. Earth's shadow falls on the Moon. The Moon turns a reddish colour — because some sunlight bends around Earth and hits the Moon. This can only happen during a full moon.
🌍
Easier to see than solar eclipses
A lunar eclipse can be seen by anyone on the night side of Earth. A solar eclipse is only visible from a narrow path on Earth's surface — that's why solar eclipses feel so rare.

Cause → Effect — how eclipses work

The Moon moves between the Sun and Earth during a new moon
→
A solar eclipse — the Moon's shadow falls on Earth
Earth moves between the Sun and the Moon during a full moon
→
A lunar eclipse — Earth's shadow falls on the Moon
The Moon's orbit is tilted 5° compared to Earth's orbit
→
Eclipses don't happen every month — the alignment must be exact
