Why are they called "tanks"?

Every word has a story. This one starts with a secret.

🔊 Read this section aloudTap to start · tap again to stop

A word born from a secret

In 1915, British engineers were building something brand new — an armoured vehicle that could roll over trenches. But they needed to keep it secret from the enemy.

When workers asked what they were building, the engineers said: "water tanks" — containers for carrying water to the front. The name stuck.

Word Origin The word tank was a code name — chosen to hide what was really being built. By the time the secret was out, everyone already called them tanks. The word has meant "armoured fighting vehicle" ever since 1916.

What did other countries call them?

Different languages invented different names — and each one reveals what the speakers thought was most important about these machines.

🇬🇧 English tank
A secret code name — chosen to look like ordinary orders for water containers. The meaning "water container" was borrowed to hide the real meaning.
🇫🇷 French char d'assaut
Char means vehicle or chariot. Assaut means assault — an attack. So French speakers named it an "assault vehicle." They were thinking about what it does.
🇩🇪 German Panzerkampfwagen
Panzer = armour · Kampf = battle · Wagen = vehicle. Put together: "armoured battle vehicle." German packed three ideas into one compound word.
🇷🇺 Russian танк (tank)
Russian simply borrowed the English word — tank — and wrote it in the Cyrillic alphabet. The English secret code name spread around the world.

🔬 Word Scientist notice

Notice this The English word tank has just one morpheme — it's a single base. But the German word Panzerkampfwagen is built from three bases joined together. Languages build words differently. English often borrows or repurposes words. German often compounds them.

As a Word Scientist, when you meet a new word, your first question is always: What does it mean? — not "how do I say it?"

The armour problem — 700 years old

Knights and tank crews had the exact same problem. How they solved it tells you everything.

🔊 Read this section aloudTap to start · tap again to stop

The same problem, 700 years apart

A medieval knight wore armour — metal plates covering the body — to survive arrows and sword blows. A tank crew in 1944 sat inside armoured steel walls for exactly the same reason: to stay alive while people were trying to kill them.

The word connecting all of this is the same word. The base is ⟨arm⟩ — and it doesn't mean the body part. It means weapons and the tools of war. It comes from Latin arma. Armour is quite literally "the weapon-stuff that covers you."

A word that travels through history arm → weapons  ·  armed → equipped with weapons  ·  armour → material that protects against weapons  ·  armament → the weapons themselves  ·  disarm → to take weapons away

The arms race — thicker armour, bigger guns

Here's the engineering nightmare of World War II: every time one side built thicker armour, the other side built a bigger gun to punch through it. It became a race.

1916 12mm of armour
The first British tanks had only 12mm of steel. Enough to stop rifle bullets. A single artillery shell could destroy them. The crew was armed — but barely protected.
1940 80mm — the T-34 breakthrough
The Soviet T-34 didn't just add thicker armour — it sloped the armour plates at an angle. A shell hitting a slope is more likely to glance off. With 80mm of armoured plate at 60°, it was harder to destroy than a tank with 120mm of flat steel.
1942 The Tiger's 120mm — too heavy to win
Germany's Tiger I had 120mm of flat armour. Incredible armament — an 88mm gun that could destroy most Allied tanks at 2km. But it weighed 57 tonnes and broke down constantly. Germany built only 1,347. The US built 49,000 Shermans. Quantity beat armour thickness.
1945 The end of the war — and a push to disarm
After the war, peace talks focused on disarmament — agreements to reduce weapons stockpiles. Countries that had spent years building armoured fleets now debated how to disarm safely without becoming vulnerable.

The sloped armour secret

Why does a T-34 beat a Tiger? It's pure geometry — and the word ⟨protect⟩ is at the centre of it.

🔊 Read this section aloudTap to start · tap again to stop

Why slope the armour?

Imagine throwing a ball at a wall. If the wall is straight up, the ball hits flat and stops. But if you tilt the wall to 45°, the ball glances off sideways. The same physics applies to shells hitting tank armour.

The Soviet T-34, introduced in 1940, had its armour plates sloped at 60°. This meant a shell didn't just have to punch through 80mm of steel — it effectively had to punch through 160mm worth of resistance, because it was hitting at an angle. The sloped plates protected the crew far better than thicker flat armour would have.

The T-34 design decision German engineers who captured T-34s in 1941 were stunned. The tank's protective geometry was so clever that Germany rushed to copy the sloped design in the Panther tank. A vehicle built for protection had changed the entire course of tank engineering.

The crew inside — what were they protecting?

A tank's armour existed for one reason: to keep the people inside alive. A standard WWII tank carried 4–5 crew: a commander, a gunner, a loader, a driver, and a radio operator. Each had one job — and the armour was their protector.

But the armour also had to be light enough to move. Too thick and the tank couldn't go anywhere. The engineers had to find the exact balance — enough protection to survive, light enough to fight. Every tank in history is a different answer to that same question.

💬 The Tiger's thick armour protected the crew — but the tank weighed so much it often sank in mud.
💬 The Sherman was the most reliable protector of Allied infantry — not because it was the most powerful, but because there were 49,000 of them.
