🧱 Stop 1 of 6 — Word Investigation Wall

What's hiding inside these words?

These words appear in today's passage about sea otters. Before you read, let's investigate what they mean — using the parts inside them as clues. Tap each word to reveal its meaning and word sum.

Word Cards

floating
🔍 Look at the base: <float>
Meaning: resting on the surface of water without sinking

Word sum:
float + ing → floating

The suffix <-ing> tells us this is happening right now. No join change is needed here — <float> ends in a consonant, so we simply add <-ing>.
wrapped
🔍 Look at the base: <wrap>
Meaning: covered or wound around something

Word sum:
wrap + ed → wrapped

Notice: the final consonant doubled at the join! <wrap> ends in a short vowel + single consonant, so we Double before adding a vowel suffix. This is the Doubling convention.
submerged
🔍 Look at the parts: <sub-> + <merge> + <-ed>
Meaning: placed completely under water

Word sum:
sub + merge + ed → submerged

The prefix <sub-> means "under" or "below." The base <merge> means "to plunge or dip." To sub-merge is to go below the surface. Notice the final <e> in <merge> was replaced when <-ed> was added — that's the Replace convention.
connected
🔍 Look at the parts: <con-> + <nect> + <-ed>
Meaning: joined or linked together

Word sum:
con + nect + ed → connected

The prefix <con-> means "together" or "with." The bound base <nect> means "bind" or "tie." To connect is to tie things together. You can see <nect> in: connect, disconnect, interconnect.
observer
🔍 Look at the parts: <ob-> + <serve> + <-er>
Meaning: one who watches carefully and notices

Word sum:
ob + serve + er → observer

The prefix <ob-> means "toward" or "over." The base <serve> here means "watch" (from Latin servare). An observer is one who watches over. The final <e> in <serve> was replaced when <-er> was added — Replace convention.
anchored
🔍 Look at the base: <anchor>
Meaning: held firmly in one place

Word sum:
anchor + ed → anchored

The suffix <-ed> tells us this happened in the past. <anchor> comes from the Greek word ankura, meaning a curved hook that holds. Sea otters anchor themselves using kelp — so they don't drift away while they sleep!
📖 Stop 2 of 6 — Two Ways to Tell a Story

Not all stories have a conflict

You may have learned that stories need a problem and a solution. Many stories do work that way — but not all of them. Today we'll look at a different kind of story: a journey narrative, where the power comes from noticing and wondering, not from conflict.

Two story structures

⚡ Conflict Structure

A character faces a problem. They struggle. They find a solution. The story ends when the problem is resolved. This creates tension and urgency — but it can also feel intense.

Example arc: Character → Problem → Struggle → Solution → Resolution

🌊 Journey Structure

A character moves through a world. They notice things. They wonder about what they see. The story ends when the journey is complete — with a feeling of calm, fullness, or understanding.

Example arc: Character → Movement → Noticing → Wondering → Arriving

The Over / Under pattern 🌊

☀️ OVER — At the Surface
  • What can be seen from above
  • Light, warmth, air
  • What the observer notices first
🌊 UNDER — Beneath the Surface
  • What lives in the hidden world below
  • Darkness, depth, quiet
  • What requires closer looking

The Passage

☀️ Over — At the Surface

Over the kelp forest, the morning is still. The water is a deep, cold green, and the light moves across it in slow, quiet patterns. A sea otter lies on her back, floating without effort. Her dark eyes are open, watching the sky.

She has wrapped a long ribbon of kelp around her body. It holds her gently in place, like a soft rope tied to the forest below. She will not drift. She does not need to swim. She simply rests, breathing the salt air, as the waves lift and lower her in a steady rhythm.

Her pup is floating beside her, tucked close, his fur still fluffy and pale from the cold. She keeps one paw resting lightly on him as they rise and fall together with the sea.

🌊 Under — Beneath the Surface

Under the kelp forest, the world is different. The light filters down in long, shifting columns — green and blue and gold — reaching toward the seafloor far below. Tall stalks of kelp rise from the rocks, connected at their roots to the stone, reaching upward toward the sun.

A purple sea urchin moves slowly across the seafloor, grazing on the kelp. It does not know that the otter above depends on this forest — and that this forest depends on the otter. Without the otter, urchins would eat every stalk. The kelp would disappear. The fish that shelter here would have nowhere to go.

But the otter dives. She is an observer first — she watches the seafloor, reading its patterns. Then she moves. She tucks her body and submerges, spiraling down through the green light, her paws tucked close, her tail pushing her deeper. She reaches the bottom, selects a sea urchin, and rises back toward the surface — back toward the air, the light, and her pup, still waiting above.

🔬 Word Scientist Spotlight

Words from the passage

float + ing → floating    (happening right now; no join change needed)
wrap + ed  → wrapped    (Doubling convention: short vowel + single consonant before vowel suffix)
connect + ed → connected (the prefix con means "together")
observ(e) + er → observer (Replace convention: final e replaced by vowel suffix)
sub + merge + s → submerges (the prefix sub means "under")

🗺️ Stop 4 of 6 — Journey Map

Mapping the Otter's Journey

A journey narrative moves through steps — not conflict and resolution, but movement and noticing. Here is the arc of the otter's story from the passage. Can you see the shape of it?

The Arc of the Journey

🌅
Step 1 — Begin
The otter wakes. She is floating, anchored in kelp. Her pup is beside her. The world is still and quiet.
👁️
Step 2 — Observe
She watches the surface world — the sky, the light, the rhythm of the waves. She is an observer first.
🌊
Step 3 — Descend
She submerges. The underwater world is revealed — kelp, light, urchins, the seafloor. The hidden world becomes visible.
🔍
Step 4 — Discover
She reads the seafloor, selects her prey, and finds what she came for. Discovery through attention, not struggle.
🌤️
Step 5 — Return
She rises back to the surface — back to the air, the light, and her pup. The journey is complete. Nothing is broken. Everything is whole.

✍️ Stop 5 of 6 — Your Journey Narrative

Write Your Own Over / Under Story 🌊

You've read a journey narrative. Now it's your turn. You'll use the same Over / Under structure to write about any animal or place you choose. There is no conflict needed — only noticing, moving, and arriving.

The Over / Under Writing Frame

☀️
Over — What does the surface world look, feel, or sound like?
Describe what can be seen, heard, or felt from above. Use sensory language — light, temperature, sound, movement.
🌊
Under — What is hidden beneath?
Reveal the world below. What lives there? What does it look like? What is surprising or interesting?
🔄
Connection — How do the two worlds depend on each other?
What connects above and below? This is where your insight lives — the moment the reader understands something new.

Your Turn 📝

You can write about the sea otter — or choose your own animal and place. Bullet points or full sentences — whatever works for you.

☀️ OVER — Describe the surface world
🌊 UNDER — Reveal the hidden world below
🔄 CONNECTION — How do the two worlds depend on each other?
🏡 RETURN — How does your journey end? What feeling does the reader leave with?

sub + merge + s → submerges    (goes under the surface)
sub + marine    → submarine     (a vessel that travels under the sea)
sub + way        → subway         (a path that goes under the ground)
