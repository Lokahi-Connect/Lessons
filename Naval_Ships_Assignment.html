Subtopic One
Destroyers
📖 Read This First
A destroyer is a fast, powerful warship designed to protect larger ships in a fleet. Destroyers are armed with guided missiles, torpedoes, and naval guns. They are built for speed — some can travel at over 30 knots (about 35 miles per hour) through open ocean. Modern destroyers carry radar and sonar systems that can detect enemy submarines lurking beneath the waves. The United States Navy operates the Arleigh Burke class, considered among the most advanced destroyers in the world. These ships are named after famous naval commanders.

Subtopic Two
Aircraft Carriers
📖 Read This First
An aircraft carrier is the largest warship ever built. It works like a floating air base, launching and recovering military jets, helicopters, and drones. The flat top deck — called the flight deck — can stretch as long as three football fields on the biggest carriers. The U.S. Navy’s Nimitz class and newer Gerald R. Ford class are powered by nuclear reactors, which means they can sail for up to 20 years without refueling. Carriers never travel alone: they move with a carrier strike group of cruisers, destroyers, and submarines that provide protection.

Subtopic Three
Submarines
📖 Read This First
A submarine is a naval vessel that can operate entirely underwater. Submarines use ballast tanks — chambers that fill with seawater to make the vessel sink, or release water to make it rise. Nuclear-powered submarines like the U.S. Navy’s Ohio class can remain submerged for months, surfacing only when the crew needs supplies. Submarines are the navy’s most secretive weapons: they can fire torpedoes to destroy other ships, or launch ballistic missiles from beneath the ocean. Their greatest advantage is stealth — an enemy often cannot detect a submarine until it is far too late.

Subtopic Four
Battleships
📖 Read This First
The battleship was once the most powerful warship on the ocean. Armored in thick steel and armed with enormous guns that could fire shells 20 miles, battleships were the centerpiece of every major navy from the early 1900s through World War II. The most famous American battleships were the Iowa class — including the USS Iowa, USS New Jersey, USS Missouri, and USS Wisconsin. The USS Missouri is especially famous: Japan signed its surrender ending World War II aboard her deck in 1945. Today no navy operates battleships — they were retired because aircraft carriers and guided missiles made them less effective. Several Iowa-class ships are now museum ships open to the public.
