Three Words, Three Meanings

Investigating there, their, and they're
Each spelling carries a different meaning — and each one has a word family to prove it.

Part 1 of 7

Three spellings. Three meanings.

These three words sound the same when you say them aloud. But look carefully — they are spelled differently. That difference is a clue. Each spelling belongs to a different word family, with a different meaning.

📍 there a place or location
🔗 they're they + are joined together
🏠 their belonging to them
🔬 Word Scientist notice: Sound alone cannot tell these apart. Their spellings carry the meaning. That is why spelling matters — it is the system working exactly as it should.
Part 2 of 7

Each word has a family

Words that share a spelling pattern — especially a shared meaningful piece — are related, like cousins in a family. Look at the spelling clues hiding in each word.

📍 The place family — words that carry <here>

here → there t + here = a place over that way
here → where wh + ere = at what place?
🔍

The hidden piece: <here>

Look at the word there. Can you see here hiding inside it?
t + here = there
The spelling <here> connects these words to the same meaning: a place, a location. That's why they're spelled the way they are.

t + here → there
wh + ere → where
All three: location words. The spelling <here> / <ere> is the shared meaning thread.

🔗 The contraction family — <they> + <are>

they + are = they're
The apostrophe ( ' ) is a visual signal. It marks the spot where letters were joined. The a in are is compressed at the join: they + are → they're. The apostrophe shows that join happened.
🔬 Notice: You can always check they're by replacing it with they are. If they are fits the sentence, then they're is the right spelling.

🏠 The belonging family — words that carry <the> + <her>

the + her → their belonging to them
🔍

Two familiar pieces in one word

The word their carries the (the article we use for specific things) and her (a belonging word). Together, they build a word that means belonging to a group. The <ir> at the end echoes other belonging markers in English.

their → carries the + ir (belonging marker)
Related to: her (her things), the (pointing to something specific)
