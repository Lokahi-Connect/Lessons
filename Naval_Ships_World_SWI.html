01
United Kingdom
HMS Queen Elizabeth
🇬🇧
📖 Read This First
The HMS Queen Elizabeth is the largest warship ever built for the Royal Navy of the United Kingdom. This enormous ship is an aircraft carrier — a floating base from which military jets can take off and land at sea. It was commissioned in 2017, which means it was officially put into active service for the first time. The ship is named after Queen Elizabeth I, who ruled England from 1558 to 1603.

The carrier weighs 65,000 tonnes and stretches 280 meters from front to back — roughly the same length as three soccer fields placed end to end. Up to 36 F-35B fighter jets can operate from its enormous flight deck. The HMS Queen Elizabeth regularly trains and sails alongside the navies of other nations, including the United States and Japan, showing that modern naval power often crosses borders.
🔬 SWI Spotlight — Word Investigation
The passage uses the word commissioned. Let’s look inside it.

It is built from two Latin parts:
   com- = together    -miss- = to send

So when a warship is commissioned, it is officially sent out together into service for the first time. This same root hides in other familiar words: mission, permission, dismiss, and submit. They all share the idea of sending something somewhere.
☕ Curious about the ship’s unusual design? (optional — click to find out) ▼
Did you know? The HMS Queen Elizabeth has two control towers on its flight deck instead of the usual one. No other carrier in the world has two islands quite like this. One tower controls the ship itself; the other manages aircraft. It was designed this way so that if one tower is damaged, the other can keep the ship running.

02
Japan — Historical
Battleship Yamato
🇯🇵
📖 Read This First
The Yamato was the most powerful battleship ever built. Launched in 1941 by the Imperial Japanese Navy, this giant warship weighed 72,800 tonnes — heavier than any battleship before or since. It stretched 263 meters from bow to stern and was armored in thick steel plating. Its nine massive guns could fire shells weighing 1,360 kilograms — almost as heavy as a small car — across a distance of 42 kilometers.

The ship was named after the ancient region of Yamato, a name that stands for Japan itself. Despite its incredible size, the Yamato was sunk on April 7, 1945, during a mission called Operation Ten-Go, when American aircraft struck it with bombs and torpedoes. Of the 2,700 sailors on board, only 280 survived. Today, the Yamato is remembered through museums, books, and films — and its name lives on in the famous Japanese animated series Space Battleship Yamato.
🔬 SWI Spotlight — Compound Words
Look at the word battleship. It is a compound word — two complete words joined together to make one new word:

   battle + ship = a ship built for battle

English builds many naval words this way. Here are a few more you might explore:
   warship (war + ship)  ·  gunboat (gun + boat)  ·  flagship (flag + ship)

A flagship was once the ship that flew a special flag to show where the admiral was. Today the word flagship still means “the most important thing of its kind” in English — even when ships are not involved at all.
☕ Want to picture just how big the Yamato was? (optional) ▼
Scale check: Each of the Yamato’s main gun barrels was 21 meters long — about as long as a school bus and a half. Each shell it fired weighed more than a fully loaded SUV. Yet even with all this firepower, the Yamato could not defend itself against aircraft attacking from above — one reason why aircraft carriers eventually replaced battleships as the most important ships in a navy.

03
India
INS Vikrant
🇮🇳
📖 Read This First
The INS Vikrant made history in 2022 as the first aircraft carrier ever designed and built entirely inside India. Before this ship, India had to buy its carriers from other countries. Now India joins a small group of nations — including the United States, the United Kingdom, France, and Russia — capable of building their own carriers.

INS stands for Indian Naval Ship. The name Vikrant comes from the ancient Sanskrit language and means “courageous.” The carrier weighs 45,000 tonnes and is 262 meters long. It can carry MiG-29K fighter jets and several types of helicopters. Building it required ten years of work and involved hundreds of Indian companies working together. When it launched, India’s Prime Minister called it a symbol of the nation’s self-reliance — the ability to build what you need for yourself.
🔬 SWI Spotlight — Where Does “Navy” Come From?
The passage mentions the Indian Naval Ship. But where does the word navy come from?

The word navy traces back to the Latin word navis, which simply meant ship:

   navis = ship  (Latin, about 2,000 years old)

Centuries ago, Latin speakers used navis for any vessel on water. The word slowly moved into French as navie (a fleet of ships), then into English as navy. But that same ancient root is hiding in many more English words we still use today — and you will discover all of them in Section 4: The Word Lab.

The Word Lab: “nav-”
🔬
🧬 How Words Actually Work
Words are not random. They have history, roots, and building blocks. When we investigate how a word is built — where it came from and what its parts mean — we understand it deeply and never have to simply memorize it.

In this section, you will investigate a single Latin word that has been quietly living inside modern English for about 2,000 years. It was used by Roman sailors and soldiers. And you use words built from it every single time you talk about ships or finding your way somewhere.
nav-
from Latin navis = ship
navy
a country’s fleet of warships and those who serve on them
naval
relating to a navy or to ships at sea
navigate
to plan and follow a route across water or land
navigator
a person whose job is to find and follow a route
navigation
the skill or process of finding and following a route
